My Design Decisions — and Why

Every screen has a reason. Here are the 4 decisions that moved the needle.

Screen · Language Select
Why is language selection the very first screen?
Users were abandoning when they hit English instructions mid-flow. Moving language selection to screen 1 — before any data entry — reduced early drop-off. If you can't read the instructions, you can't complete KYC. This is a trust moment, not a settings menu.
→ Impact: Reduced abandonment at the instruction stage
Screen · Document Capture
Why is there a frame overlay on the camera view?
First-time camera users don't know how to frame a document. The guided rectangle removes all guesswork. Combine this with real-time edge detection feedback ("Move closer", "Too dark") and you eliminate the #1 cause of failed captures — misaligned or blurry ID photos.
→ Impact: Fewer re-capture loops, faster completion
Screen · All Screens
Why is there a mic icon in the bottom corner?
The mic isn't decorative — it's voice guidance for users who cannot read. In India, 300M+ adults have low functional literacy. A KYC app that only works for literate users fails at its own job. Voice assistance makes the flow accessible without cluttering the visual hierarchy for users who don't need it.
→ Impact: Inclusive design for 300M+ low-literacy users
System · Overall Flow
Why dark UI (#3D3D3D) instead of a bright fintech look?
Bright white UIs wash out in direct sunlight — the primary environment for field agents. Dark backgrounds with high-contrast white text maintain readability outdoors. This was a deliberate environmental design choice, not an aesthetic preference.
→ Impact: Usable in real field conditions
What I'd Do Next
Add a liveness check education screen — before the selfie step, show a 3-second animated guide. Most failures happen because users don't know what "look at the camera" means on a phone.
Offline queue design — in 2G zones, capture data locally and sync when connectivity returns. The current flow assumes stable internet, which fails 40% of rural users.
Agent handoff mode — a dual-UI where the field agent sees a simplified checklist while the user sees the camera. Reduces errors from agents trying to operate the phone for users.
What I Learned
The biggest UX problems in India aren't about aesthetics — they're about literacy, connectivity, and trust. Designing for constraints is harder and more important than designing for delight.
I had to unlearn the assumption that users read instructions. Every UI decision had to work for someone who reads nothing on screen.
Research came before Figma. I spent more time understanding the drop-off points than I did designing screens — and that's what made the design actually solve something.
